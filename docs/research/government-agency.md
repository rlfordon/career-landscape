## Standard Research Findings

### What do junior/senior attorneys actually do?

**Junior agency attorneys (GS-11 to GS-13, typically 0-5 years):** Draft proposed regulations and rulemaking documents, research legal authorities for enforcement actions, write advisory opinions and guidance documents, review FOIA requests, handle administrative adjudications, prepare responses to congressional inquiries, and coordinate with other agencies on overlapping jurisdictional matters. At enforcement-heavy agencies (DOJ, SEC, FTC, EPA), juniors also conduct investigations, draft civil complaints, and manage discovery in enforcement litigation. The work is notably varied — a single junior at the EPA might draft a Clean Air Act rule in the morning and review an enforcement referral in the afternoon.

At the DOJ specifically, junior attorneys in the Civil Division handle defensive litigation (suing or being sued on behalf of the government), while those in the Criminal Division support prosecutions. U.S. Attorney's Offices function more like litigation shops, with juniors running their own cases relatively quickly.

**Senior agency attorneys (GS-14 to GS-15 / SES):** Lead rulemaking initiatives, direct enforcement strategy, prepare principals for congressional testimony and hearings, negotiate consent decrees and settlement agreements, coordinate inter-agency policy positions, manage litigation teams, and serve as subject-matter experts on complex regulatory questions. Senior counsel at agencies like the SEC or FTC often become deep specialists in specific regulatory domains (e.g., securities fraud patterns, merger analysis) with expertise that takes years to build.

**Assessment:** The dimensionality ratings (3 junior / 4 senior) are well-supported. Agency counsel work is genuinely varied — more so than most private-sector practice areas — because government lawyers must handle regulatory, advisory, enforcement, and legislative functions simultaneously. Senior counsel add policy leadership and inter-agency coordination that further broaden the role. These ratings should remain unchanged.

### What AI tools exist for this sector?

**Actual federal agency deployments (2025 inventory):**
- **DOJ:** 315 AI use cases logged in the 2025 federal AI inventory, a 31% increase from 2024. The Civil Division expanded from 1 use case to 17, including evidence synthesis, claim consolidation, legal research automation, and AI-generated content detection. The Executive Office for U.S. Attorneys uses Palantir for case information integration and analysis (deployed June 2025). The Civil Division also uses Salesforce-based AI workflows.
- **Federal Bureau of Prisons:** BRAVO classification system predicts misconduct risk; PATTERN tool analyzes recidivism risk.
- **FBI/U.S. Marshals:** Facial recognition via Amazon Rekognition and Clearview AI for investigative and identification purposes.
- **Government-wide:** The 2024 consolidated federal AI inventory listed 2,133 publicly reportable use cases across 41 agencies. Of DOJ's 315, 114 were classified "high-impact" (affecting legal rights and safety).

**eDiscovery and document review platforms:**
- **Casepoint:** The only legal tech provider with FedRAMP High and DOD IL5/IL6 authorization. A major federal law enforcement agency with 1,300+ attorneys migrated 65 TB of data (224 workspaces) from Relativity to Casepoint. A major defense agency uses it for eDiscovery, FOIA, litigation holds, and Congressional inquiries.
- **Relativity (RelOne Gov):** FedRAMP-authorized eDiscovery platform widely used across federal agencies, though sunsetting its on-premises Server product by end of 2027, forcing agencies to migrate to cloud.

**General legal AI tools:** Lexis+ AI and Westlaw AI are available to federal attorneys for legal research, though adoption varies by agency and budget.

**Assessment:** AI tool deployment in federal agencies is real but uneven. The DOJ is the most advanced adopter, with 315 use cases. But most agencies lag significantly. The Biden-era AI Executive Order (EO 14110, Oct 2023) had mandated Chief AI Officers, use-case inventories, and structured oversight — but Trump rescinded it on Day 1 (Jan 20, 2025) and replaced it with EO 14179, focused on deregulation of AI rather than government adoption. The institutional infrastructure for government AI adoption has been disrupted even as the tools exist.

### What are clients/counterparties doing with AI?

Government agency counsel do not have "clients" in the traditional sense — the public and the agency itself are the clients. But several dynamics matter:

**Regulated entities are adopting AI aggressively.** Companies subject to SEC, FTC, EPA, and CFPB oversight are deploying AI for compliance monitoring, regulatory filings, and enforcement response. This creates a growing asymmetry: regulated entities with AI-augmented legal teams facing enforcement actions brought by agencies with shrinking, under-resourced legal staffs.

**The plaintiffs' bar is using AI too.** In areas where agencies share enforcement space with private litigants (securities fraud, consumer protection, environmental), private attorneys using AI tools like EvenUp and CoCounsel can now match or exceed the throughput of junior agency lawyers.

**Congress and oversight bodies** generate increasing demands for agency legal analysis — testimony preparation, regulatory impact assessments, responses to investigations — that create workload pressure regardless of enforcement levels.

**Assessment:** The asymmetry between AI-equipped regulated entities and under-resourced agencies is a meaningful new dynamic. It does not change the self-service resistance rating (the government's monopoly on regulation is real) but it does affect the practical effectiveness of agency counsel and could create political pressure to either increase staffing or accept diminished enforcement.

### Industry commentary on AI impact

- **National Law Review (Jan 2026), "85 Predictions for AI and the Law in 2026":** Predicts state attorneys general will become primary AI enforcers as federal enforcement retreats, with Illinois, Texas, and Colorado implementing AI workforce laws in 2026.
- **FedScoop (2025):** DOJ's 31% increase in AI use cases reflects genuine adoption momentum, but notes that "high-impact" deployments (affecting rights) require careful governance that the rescission of EO 14110 has undermined.
- **Gibson Dunn, SEC Enforcement Year-End Update (2025):** Documents SEC enforcement actions falling to 313 — lowest in a decade, down 27% from FY 2024 — with the note that "reduced workforce capacity and reorganization efforts have likely contributed to the recent decline."
- **Harvard Law School Center on the Legal Profession (2025):** Found that even AmLaw 100 firms reporting 100x productivity gains on specific tasks do not anticipate reducing attorney headcount. This suggests AI augmentation rather than replacement — but this finding applies to private-sector firms with market incentives, not government agencies with political budget pressures.
- **McKinsey (2025):** Estimates 22% of a lawyer's job can be automated today, with 44% of legal tasks technically automatable. Government legal work, with its heavy regulatory drafting and research components, likely sits above these averages.

## Sector-Specific Findings

### Is the Decision-Maker Alignment rating of 2 too generous?

**Yes. The evidence strongly supports lowering this to 1, at least for the current political environment.**

The data is stark:

- **8,599 licensed attorneys left the federal government** between January 2025 and November 2025, resulting in a net decline of 6,524 federal lawyers (OPM data via JD Journal, Jan 2026).
- **The SEC's workforce is down 15%**, with the Enforcement Division requesting only 1,178 FTE for FY 2026 — down 17% from 1,424 actual FTE in FY 2024. SEC enforcement actions fell to 313, the lowest in a decade. Monetary settlements dropped 45% to $808 million (Paul Weiss, 2026).
- **The CFPB lost 86% of its workforce** (1,500 of ~1,700 employees laid off), with plans to reduce to 5 employees. Courts blocked some cuts but the Supreme Court (8-1, July 2025) ultimately allowed workforce reductions to proceed.
- **The EPA dismissed 300+ staff**, including regulatory enforcement officials.
- **The DOJ's Consumer Protection Branch was dissolved entirely** as part of a headquarters reorganization, and DOJ experienced a net reduction of ~8,900 total employees.
- **The FY 2026 federal pay raise is 1%** with locality pay frozen — well below inflation and widening the gap with private-sector legal salaries. This accelerates voluntary departures.
- **Government-wide:** ~300,000 federal layoffs announced; ~200,000 had departed by August 2025; 9% of the federal workforce eliminated by March 2026.

The current description says "Agencies with broad political support are safer. Those with contested missions face AI-as-budget-cut-cover." This understates what is happening. The cuts are not primarily AI-driven — they are politically driven workforce reductions that happen to coincide with AI availability. AI is not being deployed as a substitute; positions are simply being eliminated. The "AI-as-budget-cut-cover" framing implies AI is the proximate cause, when the actual mechanism is direct political hostility to the regulatory state.

**However**, this is clearly a cyclical political swing, not a permanent structural change. Courts have pushed back (multiple injunctions, even if the Supreme Court allowed many cuts). Future administrations will likely rebuild. The question is whether the rating should reflect the current moment or the long-term average.

**Recommendation:** Lower Decision-Maker Alignment to 1 for both junior and senior, with descriptions that acknowledge the cyclical nature but emphasize the severity of the current moment. A rating of 2 implies decision-makers are merely indifferent; the current environment is actively hostile.

### Is "Political Variable" too neutral a verdict?

**For the current moment, yes — but as a long-term assessment, it remains appropriate.**

The "Political Variable" verdict is the right structural category. Government agency counsel careers genuinely swing between expansion (Obama/Biden regulatory agendas) and contraction (Trump/DOGE deregulatory agendas) based on political control. No other legal sector has this degree of exogenous political determination.

But the current swing is more extreme than previous cycles:
- The DOGE-era cuts are larger in absolute terms than any prior administration's workforce reductions.
- The Supreme Court's July 2025 decision allowing mass layoffs removed a judicial check that had previously moderated political swings.
- The rescission of EO 14110 dismantled the institutional infrastructure for responsible AI adoption in government, meaning AI is available as a justification for cuts without being deployed as a genuine capability replacement.

**Recommendation:** Keep "Political Variable" as the verdict — it correctly identifies the structural dynamic. But update the descriptions to convey the severity and asymmetry of the current political moment. The verdictType of "political" is correct.

### What AI tools are agencies actually deploying?

**Real deployments exist, but they are concentrated at DOJ and are mostly investigative/enforcement tools rather than legal practice tools.**

The DOJ's 315 AI use cases are genuine. But most are investigative (facial recognition, predictive analytics, surveillance) rather than lawyer-productivity tools (legal research, drafting, document review). The Civil Division's expansion from 1 to 17 use cases is meaningful but small-scale. Palantir integration for case management at the Executive Office for U.S. Attorneys is a real deployment that affects how junior attorneys work.

Casepoint's FedRAMP High authorization and adoption by major federal law enforcement agencies represents genuine eDiscovery modernization. But this is document review infrastructure, not the kind of AI that replaces attorney judgment.

**The critical gap:** The Biden-era AI governance infrastructure (Chief AI Officers, structured inventories, risk frameworks) was dismantled by EO 14179. Agencies that were building systematic AI adoption plans lost their institutional mandate. Individual tool deployments continue, but the coordinated push for AI-augmented government legal work has stalled.

**Assessment:** The Focus Effect rating of 3 for juniors remains appropriate — AI can meaningfully assist with regulatory research and drafting. But the description should note that actual deployment is lagging behind capability, partly due to the disruption of federal AI governance infrastructure.

### How do state agency counsel differ from federal?

**State agency counsel are on a meaningfully different trajectory from federal.**

- **State AGs are actively hiring.** Multiple state attorney general offices (NY, NJ, MN, TX) have open positions in early 2026. Texas AG alone has 4,000+ employees.
- **State AGs are becoming primary AI enforcers.** With federal enforcement retreating, states are stepping into the gap. Illinois, Texas, and Colorado are implementing AI workforce laws in 2026 with state AG enforcement authority. A bipartisan task force of state AGs launched in 2025 to coordinate AI policy.
- **State regulatory workload is growing.** 11 new state privacy laws took effect in 2025-2026. State AGs issued AI guidance proactively (NJ in Jan 2025, CA in Jan 2025). The federal-state tension over AI regulation (Trump's EO directing DOJ to challenge state AI laws) actually creates more legal work for state counsel, not less.
- **State budgets are set differently.** State appropriations are less susceptible to the kind of executive-driven DOGE-style cuts that have devastated federal agencies. Governors can cut, but the mechanism is slower and more politically constrained.

**Assessment:** The current profile treats "Government Agency Counsel" as a monolith. The federal/state split is significant enough to note in descriptions. State counsel are in a structurally stronger position than federal counsel in the current environment — growing workload, active hiring, and insulation from DOGE-style executive cuts.

**Rationale for Decision-Maker Alignment change (2 to 1, both levels):** A rating of 2 means decision-makers are somewhat misaligned. The current environment is actively hostile: 6,524 net federal attorney departures in 10 months, SEC enforcement staff down 17%, CFPB gutted to 86% workforce loss, DOJ Consumer Protection Branch dissolved, and a 1% pay raise that accelerates voluntary attrition. The appropriation process is being used to shrink agencies, not maintain them. This is not indifference — it is active opposition. The "political variable" verdict correctly captures the cyclical nature, but the rating should reflect the current severity. When the political environment shifts, this rating can be revisited upward.
