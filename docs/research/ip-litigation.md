## Standard Research Findings

### What do junior/senior attorneys actually do?

**Junior associates (1-4 years):** Work depends heavily on case stage. Early-stage work involves prior art review and product analysis — identifying relevant prior art patents and mapping accused product functionality against patent claims. Mid-stage work is the grind: written discovery, drafting infringement and invalidity contentions, claim chart preparation (comparing patent claim elements against product documentation, user manuals, and marketing materials), and expert witness coordination. Pre-trial work includes deposition preparation, summary judgment briefing, and examination outlines.

IP litigation teams are typically lean — a partner, one senior associate, and one junior — meaning juniors get broader exposure than in general commercial litigation. Juniors interact with expert witnesses early and regularly. Over 80% of patent litigators at major firms hold technical degrees (engineering, computer science, life sciences), and juniors are expected to leverage that technical background to understand the accused technology and patent claims.

However, a significant portion of junior time goes to tasks that are structurally similar to general litigation: document review, discovery responses, and the mechanical aspects of claim chart preparation (mapping claim language to evidence, often across thousands of pages of technical documentation). The "technical knowledge" required varies enormously by matter — a semiconductor fabrication patent requires genuine engineering expertise, while a business method patent may require little more than reading comprehension.

**Senior attorneys/partners:** Combine legal strategy (forum selection, claim construction positioning, settlement/licensing negotiation), technical mastery (working with experts, understanding the technology deeply enough to cross-examine opposing experts), courtroom advocacy (Markman hearings, trial), and client counseling (portfolio strategy, enforcement decisions, licensing programs). They also manage ITC proceedings and PTAB/IPR strategy, which require different procedural expertise.

**Assessment:** Junior dimensionality of 3 is defensible but generous. The technical knowledge requirement is real and distinguishes IP lit from general litigation, but the actual day-to-day task set of a junior — claim charts, prior art search, discovery, document review — is narrower than the description implies. Much of the "engaging with technical subject matter deeply" happens at the senior level; juniors more often execute mechanical mapping tasks that require enough technical literacy to follow the technology but not enough to qualify as truly high-dimensional work. The senior rating of 4 is well-supported — it is genuinely one of the highest-dimensional litigation specialties.

### What AI tools exist for this sector?

**Claim chart and litigation tools:**
- **Harvey AI:** Published five workflow templates in 2025 targeting patent litigation: drafting infringement claim charts, analyzing patent office actions, generating invalidity contentions, preparing license agreements, and populating filing documents. The claim chart template automates initial mapping of patent claims to evidence, citing specific page numbers and flagging uncertain elements. Harvey reports that "a junior associate running the claim chart workflow gets the same structural output as a senior partner would expect, with citations already in place."
- **Patlytics:** AI-native patent intelligence platform covering 70M+ patent publications and 250M+ non-patent literature sources. Generates claim-by-claim, element-by-element invalidity charts. Raised $14M Series A (led by Next47 with Google's Gradient Ventures). Am Law 100 firms report 80% time savings on patent counseling projects and $38,000 in additional profit per engagement. Pricing estimated at $800-$2,000/user/month.
- **&AI (tryandai.com):** AI platform for scaling patent expertise, focused on claim chart generation and patent analysis.

**Prior art search tools:**
- **PatSnap:** AI-powered patent search and R&D competitive intelligence. Enterprise-tier pricing.
- **Ambercite:** AI similarity and relevance ranking for prior art, claiming 46% improvement in search quality over traditional methods.
- **Clarivate Derwent Innovation:** Patent search with AI-powered citation mapping and claims analysis.
- **NLPatent:** Claims 80% reduction in time for patent searching with real-time AI analysis.
- **Lens.org:** Free/freemium access to 115M patents and 200M scholarly works.

**Litigation analytics:**
- **Lex Machina (LexisNexis):** Patent litigation analytics covering 130,000+ U.S. IP cases, with data on 1,400+ judges, 340,000+ litigants. Patent similarity engine identifies non-obvious related patents. Used for judge selection, opposing counsel profiling, and outcome prediction.
- **Docket Navigator:** Patent litigation data and analytics, particularly strong on ITC proceedings.

**Assessment:** The tool landscape is maturing rapidly, and the tools that exist target precisely the work juniors do most: claim chart drafting, prior art searching, and invalidity analysis. Harvey's explicit framing — that its tool gives a junior the same output as a senior expects — is a tell. These tools don't eliminate juniors, but they dramatically compress the hours a junior bills on core tasks. The 80% time savings figure reported by multiple platforms is significant if even partially accurate.

### What are clients/counterparties doing with AI?

**Corporate IP departments** are the primary clients for patent litigation. Unlike insurance defense, where carriers actively use AI to squeeze outside counsel, corporate IP clients are not (yet) building litigation tools in-house. However:

- **In-house AI adoption is growing:** The LexisNexis/Forrester 2025 study found in-house legal teams using AI could reduce outside counsel spend by 13% ($602K+ over 3 years). Corporate legal departments are "adopting GenAI faster than outside counsel" per Law.com (Dec 2025).
- **NPE/PAE plaintiffs** are using AI tools to scale assertion campaigns. Patent trolls accounted for nearly 70% of AI-related patent lawsuits in recent years. RPX and Unified Patents provide AI-powered analytics to defendants to counter NPE strategies.
- **AI companies as defendants** are generating entirely new categories of IP litigation (see Sector-Specific Findings below).

**Assessment:** Client-side AI pressure is less acute than in insurance defense — corporate IP clients aren't building CaseGlide-like oversight tools. But the 13% outside-counsel reduction trend applies here too, and sophisticated corporate clients will increasingly expect AI-driven efficiency and resist paying for junior hours on tasks AI tools can handle.

### Industry commentary on AI impact

- **Above the Law (March 2026):** BigLaw partners acknowledge "scale armies are dead in the water. AI is already obliterating the high-leverage model." Firms have started recruiting fewer associates at junior levels. The associate-to-partner ratio declined from ~2:1 in 2000 to 1.3:1 in 2025, with two-thirds of large firms expecting AI to further influence leverage ratios by 2035.
- **IPWatchdog (September 2025):** In a survey of nearly 500 IP industry professionals, 43% identified "the growing reliance on AI in litigation strategy" as having the greatest impact on patent litigation.
- **Harvard Law School CLP (2025):** Firms are not yet reducing total associate headcount, but are not expanding first-year or summer associate classes. Entry-level groups, long the foundation of the BigLaw pyramid, face reduced demand as AI automates routine tasks.
- **Artificial Lawyer Predictions 2026:** "The traditional pyramid model is going to go the way of the dodo," though litigation boutiques may be an exception.

## Sector-Specific Findings

### Is "Well Protected" for juniors actually justified?

**The technical knowledge defense is real but overstated.** Yes, IP litigation requires technical literacy — understanding patent claims, accused product functionality, and prior art. Over 80% of patent litigators hold STEM degrees. But the question is whether this technical requirement makes junior work resistant to AI, and the evidence says: less than the current profile assumes.

The core junior tasks — claim chart preparation, prior art searching, document review, and invalidity analysis — are precisely where AI tools are showing the strongest results:

- Harvey's claim chart workflow explicitly produces output that matches "what a senior partner would expect," with citations in place — this is a junior associate's primary value-add.
- Patlytics and similar tools deliver 80% time savings on prior art searches and patent counseling projects.
- AI-powered invalidity analysis can generate limitation-by-limitation charts with citations to Section 102 and 103 prior art, reducing what took weeks to hours.

The technical knowledge that genuinely protects juniors is case-specific judgment: understanding which claim construction arguments are strongest, which prior art references are most analogous, how an expert witness should frame the technology. But much of what juniors actually do — the mechanical mapping of claim language to evidence across thousands of document pages — is high-volume, pattern-matching work where AI excels.

**Assessment:** The junior Focus Effect rating of 2 ("some routine tasks automated, but specialized nature means juniors still add significant value") is too optimistic. A rating of 1 is too harsh — juniors do add genuine technical judgment that pure AI cannot replicate. But the current description understates how much of the junior role is claim chart grinding and prior art searching, both of which are being compressed by 50-80%. **Recommend changing junior Focus Effect from 2 to 1.** The "significant value on technical analysis" framing masks the reality that most junior hours go to tasks AI tools now handle.

Similarly, junior Dimensionality of 3 is generous. The task set — claim charts, prior art, discovery, document review — is broader than insurance defense (2) but narrower than the description implies. The technical knowledge adds a genuine dimension, but the actual work product is more repetitive than "engaging with technical subject matter deeply" suggests. **Recommend keeping at 3** but rewriting the description to be more honest about what juniors actually do versus what the practice area theoretically requires.

### Is AI creating NEW categories of IP litigation?

**Yes, significantly.** This is the strongest demand-side factor for the practice area.

**Volume:** The number of AI-related IP cases more than doubled from ~30 at end of 2024 to over 70 by end of 2025. Debevoise tracks 50+ pending federal lawsuits between IP holders and AI developers. Major cases include:
- Copyright training data disputes: NYT v. Microsoft/OpenAI, Concord Music v. Anthropic ($3.1B claim, Jan 2026), UMG v. Suno, In re Google Generative AI Litigation, Disney/Universal v. Midjourney (Jun 2025)
- Trademark/Lanham Act theories: Encyclopaedia Britannica v. Perplexity (Sep 2025), OverDrive v. OpenAI (Sora trademark), Cameo v. OpenAI
- AI inventorship: Thaler v. Vidal line of cases (Supreme Court denied cert Mar 2026), ongoing policy uncertainty with USPTO's "don't ask, don't tell" approach under Director Squires (Nov 2025)

**Settlements as market signal:** The $1.5B Bartz v. Anthropic settlement was the biggest AI IP development of 2025, joined by Warner Music settlements with Udio and Suno. These settlements signal that AI IP litigation has real teeth and real value for IP holders.

**Expanding theories:** Debevoise projects 2026 will bring "sharper challenges to fair-use defenses," "aggressive plaintiff strategies to unlock proprietary training information through discovery," and "new wave of class certification battles." Litigation is shifting from training-data disputes to output-based infringement claims, a broader theory.

**NPE activity in AI patents:** NPEs filed 70% of AI-related patent lawsuits, with a 15-20% increase in NPE activity in 2025 Q1-Q2 versus 2024. Reduced PTAB discretionary denials are motivating more NPE filings by reducing the chance of stays in district court.

**Assessment:** The demand elasticity rating of 2 for both juniors and seniors understates the demand growth story. Patent litigation filings surged 22% in 2024 (Lex Machina), with record $4.3B in damages. AI-related IP disputes are an entirely new litigation category that didn't meaningfully exist 3 years ago. ITC filings rose 56% YoY in 2024. The IP litigation market is projected to grow at 7% CAGR to $30B+ by 2033. **Recommend changing demand elasticity to 3 for both junior and senior,** with descriptions that specifically cite AI-as-subject-matter litigation and NPE growth.

### How does the ITC/PTAB split affect things?

**PTAB/IPR proceedings** are more streamlined than district court litigation — faster timeline (12-18 months), no jury, limited discovery, focused on invalidity. This makes them potentially more susceptible to AI-assisted prior art searching and invalidity analysis. However, the PTAB is also becoming more favorable to patent owners: reduced discretionary denials under current leadership are pushing more disputes into district court, which is less AI-automatable (jury trials, broader discovery, Markman hearings).

**ITC proceedings** (Section 337) are growing — 48 complaints filed in FY2025, up from a low point. The ITC has limited jurisdiction over purely digital/AI imports (Section 337 applies to "articles"), but AI-enabled hardware (semiconductors, sensors, wearables) generates substantial ITC work. Win rates for complainants improved to 58% in 2025, above the 10-year average.

**Assessment:** The ITC/PTAB split doesn't dramatically change the overall picture. PTAB work is more automatable but is also being displaced into district court. ITC work is growing but remains a small slice. Neither venue materially changes the ratings.

### What's happening with NPE litigation and AI?

NPE/PAE activity is the primary growth driver in patent litigation volume. Unified Patents data shows NPE filings increased 15-20% in Q1-Q2 2025 versus 2024, contributing to the overall 20% uptick in patent litigation. The operating company filing rate has remained stable — all growth comes from PAEs.

NPEs account for nearly 70% of AI-related patent lawsuits, making AI patents a prime assertion target because machine learning and NLP technologies are used across industries (broad potential defendant pool).

Defense-side tools like RPX and Unified Patents provide AI-powered analytics to help defendants assess NPE patent strength and coordinate responses. But NPE growth is net positive for IP litigation demand — more cases mean more work for IP litigators on both sides.

**Assessment:** NPE growth reinforces the case for upgrading demand elasticity. The current description ("patent disputes and competitive dynamics") doesn't capture this dynamic.

**Rationale for junior Focus Effect downgrade (2 -> 1):** AI tools (Harvey, Patlytics, NLPatent) are delivering 50-80% time savings on the exact tasks that consume most junior hours: claim chart preparation, prior art searching, invalidity analysis. Harvey explicitly markets its claim chart workflow as producing output equivalent to what a senior partner expects from a junior. The "specialized nature" of the work does not protect juniors from AI compression the way the current rating implies — the specialization is in the tool's training data, not only in the associate's head.

**Rationale for demand elasticity upgrade (2 -> 3):** Patent litigation filings surged 22% in 2024, with record $4.3B in damages. AI-related IP disputes have created an entirely new case category (70+ pending federal cases, up from ~30 in 2024). NPE activity grew 15-20% in 2025 and NPEs account for 70% of AI patent assertions. ITC filings rose 56% YoY. The IP litigation market is projected for 7% CAGR growth through 2033.

**Rationale for junior verdict change:** With Focus Effect dropping to 1 and Demand Elasticity rising to 3, the net picture is more nuanced than "Well Protected." Juniors benefit from rising demand but face significant compression of their core billable tasks. The demand growth may not translate into proportional junior hiring — firms can handle more cases with fewer juniors when each junior is 50-80% more efficient on claim charts and prior art. This is a "Mixed Outlook" scenario: the work exists but the headcount per case is shrinking.

### Description Updates

**Junior dimensionality** — More honest about what juniors actually do:
> "Patent litigation requires technical literacy (80%+ of IP litigators hold STEM degrees), but junior work centers on claim chart preparation, prior art searching, discovery, and document review. The technical dimension is real — understanding accused products and patent claims requires genuine STEM knowledge — but the day-to-day task set is narrower than the practice area's reputation suggests."

**Junior focus effect** — Reflect that AI tools directly target junior work:
> "AI tools now automate the core junior tasks: Harvey's claim chart workflow produces partner-ready output with citations, Patlytics delivers 80% time savings on prior art and invalidity analysis. The specialized technical knowledge that juniors bring is being embedded in tools, compressing billable hours on the work that defines the junior role."

**Junior demand elasticity** — Cite the growth story specifically:
> "Patent litigation filings surged 22% in 2024 with record $4.3B in damages. AI-related IP disputes are an entirely new case category — 70+ federal cases pending, up from ~30 in 2024. NPE activity grew 15-20% in 2025. But rising demand may not mean proportional junior hiring: AI-driven efficiency means fewer junior hours per case even as case volume grows."

**Junior decision-maker alignment** — Sharpen the tension:
> "Partners want revenue from IP disputes, and rising case volume delivers. But AI tools that compress junior hours create a leverage problem: partners can staff fewer juniors per case while maintaining margins. Clients increasingly expect AI-driven efficiency and will resist paying junior rates for claim charts AI tools can draft."

**Senior demand elasticity** — Cite specific growth drivers:
> "AI-as-subject-matter litigation is a genuine growth category: 70+ pending AI IP cases, $1.5B Anthropic settlement, Disney/Universal suing Midjourney, $3.1B Concord Music claim. NPE filings grew 15-20% in 2025. ITC complaints rose 56% YoY. This is one of the few litigation sectors with a strong, evidence-based demand growth story."

**Senior focus effect** — Add nuance about how AI helps seniors:
> "AI tools enhance senior workflow rather than threatening it — litigation analytics (Lex Machina), prior art verification, and claim chart review become faster. Seniors spend freed time on claim construction strategy, expert management, and trial preparation. The efficiency gains accrue to the senior, not against them."
