## Standard Research Findings

### What do junior/senior attorneys actually do?

**Junior associates (1-4 years):** The bulk of junior mass tort work is intake and case development — a pipeline-processing role rather than traditional litigation. Day-to-day tasks include:

- **Intake screening:** Evaluating potential claimants against exposure criteria (product use dates, dosage, duration) and diagnosis criteria (specific injuries, medical confirmation). Firms may screen tens of thousands of leads per tort.
- **Plaintiff Fact Sheets (PFS):** Completing court-ordered questionnaires for each plaintiff — gathering medical records, treatment histories, product usage details, and employment/exposure information. PFS completion is a massive, deadline-driven administrative burden.
- **Medical chronology assembly:** Reviewing medical records to build timelines linking product exposure to injury. This is the most substantive junior task but remains highly structured.
- **Document management:** Organizing discovery productions, medical records, and client files across inventories of thousands of cases.
- **Client communication:** Scheduling calls with plaintiffs to complete fact sheets, obtaining medical authorizations, and gathering supporting documentation.

Juniors do not typically take depositions, try cases, or coordinate with experts in mass tort practice. Those tasks belong to senior attorneys on the Plaintiff Steering Committee or bellwether trial teams. The junior role is narrower than in other plaintiff-side litigation areas (e.g., single-event PI, where a junior might handle a case start-to-finish within a year or two).

**Senior attorneys/partners:** The senior role in mass tort is genuinely multidimensional:

- **MDL leadership:** Competing for and serving on Plaintiff Steering Committees (PSCs), which control litigation strategy, expert selection, and settlement negotiations for entire MDLs. PSC positions carry enormous influence and fee multipliers.
- **Bellwether trial preparation and trial:** Selecting, preparing, and trying representative cases that set settlement benchmarks for the entire MDL. TorHoerman Law's $495M NEC verdict against Abbott Labs is a recent example.
- **Expert witness management:** Retaining and coordinating medical experts, epidemiologists, and regulatory specialists for Daubert hearings and trial.
- **Inventory management:** Strategic decisions about which torts to invest in, how many cases to acquire, when to settle inventories vs. push to trial.
- **Settlement negotiation:** Negotiating global settlements or inventory-specific deals, often involving complex allocation formulas across thousands of plaintiffs.
- **Litigation finance relationships:** Securing and managing third-party funding arrangements (the litigation finance market hit $19.3B in 2025, with mass torts being a primary target).

**Assessment:** Junior dimensionality of 2 is accurate — the role is narrower than the profile's current description suggests, centering on intake processing rather than "case management" broadly. Senior dimensionality of 4 is well-supported; PSC leadership, bellwether trials, inventory strategy, and settlement negotiation are genuinely distinct, high-complexity dimensions.

### What AI tools exist for this sector?

The plaintiff mass tort sector has attracted significant VC investment in purpose-built AI tools — far more than insurance defense:

**Case origination and harm detection:**
- **Darrow** ($91M total funding, $60M Series B in April 2025): Uses AI to scan billions of data points — adverse event reports, medical literature, regulatory filings — to identify mass tort opportunities before they become widely known. Connects plaintiff firms with qualified plaintiffs through targeted marketing and vetting. This is a genuinely new capability: AI-powered case origination that did not exist before.
- **Justpoint** ($105M in funding): First AI-native mass tort law firm, approved as an Alternative Business Structure in Arizona (July 2025). Processes 1,000+ claims daily. Flagged 15 drugs with underreported adverse events. Detected dangers in Oxbryta 21 months before FDA withdrawal — compressing detection timelines from decades to months.

**Medical record analysis and case intelligence:**
- **Supio** ($91M total funding, $60M Series B in April 2025): AI platform specifically for PI and mass tort firms. Helped TorHoerman Law transform 43,000+ pages of medical records and 600+ hours of depositions into trial-ready insights for a $495M verdict. Thomas Law Offices reports 62% increase in annual case volume since adoption. Claims 85%+ reduction in document review time. 4x ARR growth since August 2024 launch.
- **EvenUp**: AI-generated demand packages for PI/mass tort. Processes ~10,000 cases per week. Claims 69% higher likelihood of hitting policy limits. Uses database of 250,000+ verdicts and settlements for valuation benchmarking.

**Intake and screening:**
- **Pattern Data**: AI-powered mass tort case screening with 98% accuracy on medical record review. 10x faster case reviews. Objective scoring to evaluate intake quality and predict case viability. HIPAA-compliant. Uses RAG approach for medical record analysis.
- **Litify**: Enterprise case management on Salesforce with AI-driven lead scoring and automated qualification for mass tort intake. $4,000-$10,000/month.
- **Assembly/Neos**: Cloud-based case management with AI document generation and summaries for mass tort firms.

**General tools with mass tort applications:**
- **SmartAdvocate** (created by Parker Waichman founder Jerry Parker): AI-powered case summarization for mass tort.

**Assessment:** The AI tool ecosystem for plaintiff mass tort is deep, well-funded, and growing fast. Total known VC investment in plaintiff-side mass tort AI exceeds $280M (Darrow $91M + Supio $91M + Justpoint $105M). This dwarfs investment in defense-side legal AI tools. Critically, these tools target the exact tasks juniors perform (intake screening, medical record review, PFS completion), not senior work (trial strategy, PSC leadership, settlement negotiation).

### What are clients/counterparties doing with AI?

In plaintiff mass tort, the relevant "counterparty" is the defense side — pharmaceutical companies, medical device manufacturers, and their defense counsel.

**Defense-side AI adoption:**
The research found surprisingly little evidence of defense-specific AI tools designed to counter plaintiff mass tort advantages. Defense firms face the same structural barriers as insurance defense: their clients (corporate defendants) control the purse strings and are incentivized to minimize outside counsel spend, not invest in defense AI.

However, defense-side concerns are emerging:
- Defense advocates worry that AI enables "building inventory" — accumulating thousands of claimants "meritorious and otherwise" to maximize settlement pressure regardless of claim quality (Winston & Strawn, 2025).
- The MDL Subcommittee estimated 20-30% (and possibly up to 50%) of claims in mass tort MDLs may be marginal or frivolous. AI-powered intake could exacerbate this by reducing the cost of filing claims.
- U.S. District Judge M. Casey Rodgers issued a May 2025 order in the Depo-Provera MDL requiring specific pleading standards ("Requisite Physical Injury") and threatening sanctions for deficient complaints.

**Judicial response:**
Courts are beginning to impose quality controls that could constrain AI-driven claim inflation:
- Mandatory early disclosure of basic verifying information
- Tying PSC leadership appointments to claim quality standards
- Penalties for firms filing unsupportable claims
- Calls for mandatory Rule 11 sanctions

**Assessment:** The defense side has not developed AI tools comparable to plaintiff-side innovations. This asymmetry is real and significant. However, judicial backlash against inflated inventories represents a genuine constraint on unlimited growth through AI-powered intake.

## Sector-Specific Findings

### Is the "Growth" verdict for juniors too optimistic given focusEffect of 2?

This is the most important tension in the current profile. The focusEffect of 2 correctly identifies that AI automates what juniors actually do (intake, PFS completion, medical record review) rather than freeing them for higher-value complementary work. But the verdict says "Growth."

**The resolution lies in the other dimensions.** In most sectors, a focusEffect of 2 would signal vulnerability. But mass tort is unusual:

1. **Demand elasticity is genuinely high (4).** AI-powered intake lets firms screen dramatically more claimants. Supio customers report 62% increases in annual case volume. Justpoint processes 1,000+ claims daily. Pattern Data achieves 10x faster case reviews. More cases require more humans in the loop — even if each human handles more cases.

2. **Decision-maker alignment is genuinely high (4).** Partners on contingency capture 33-40% of every dollar recovered. Every efficiency gain flows to more cases and higher profits — no client is pressuring fee reductions.

3. **Self-service resistance is genuinely high (4).** Injured plaintiffs cannot self-serve their way through pharmaceutical MDL litigation.

**However, the growth story has limits.** The 69% of paralegal billable work that could be automated suggests the growth in case volume must outpace the per-case labor reduction. The evidence currently supports this — firms are expanding intake, not shrinking staff — but this is an equilibrium that could shift.

**Assessment:** The "Growth" verdict is defensible but should be understood as "net growth despite automation of core tasks," driven by massive demand expansion. The focusEffect of 2 is correct and should not change. But the junior verdict is more nuanced than "Growth" might suggest — it is growth in total positions but a transformation of the role toward higher-volume oversight rather than hands-on case work.

### How real is the Jevons paradox claim?

**The evidence for Jevons paradox in mass tort is stronger than in any other legal sector, but not unlimited.**

**Evidence supporting demand expansion:**
- Thomas Law Offices reports 62% increase in annual case volume after adopting Supio.
- Justpoint processes 1,000+ claims daily with AI, having identified 15 drugs with underreported adverse events — these are genuinely new cases that would not have been found without AI.
- Darrow's entire business model is AI-powered case origination — discovering mass torts before they become widely known.
- Litigation finance commitments hit $2.7B in new funding in FY2023, with AI making previously marginal cases financeable.
- MDLs represent ~65% of all federal civil cases, with ~197,000 actions pending as of FY2025.
- The litigation funding market is projected to grow from $19.3B (2025) to $53.2B (2035).

**Evidence limiting demand expansion:**
- The MDL Subcommittee estimates 20-50% of mass tort claims may be marginal or frivolous. AI-inflated inventories are attracting judicial scrutiny.
- Judge Rodgers' Depo-Provera order (May 2025) imposes heightened pleading standards and sanctions for deficient claims — a template other MDL judges may follow.
- The defense bar and reform advocates (ILR) are pushing legislative changes: mandatory Rule 11 sanctions, early disclosure requirements, and PSC appointment standards tied to claim quality.
- The Jevons paradox skeptics have a point: "Lowering the cost of legal work does not reveal new legal problems — it just changes who, or what, handles the ones that already exist." For mass tort specifically, the number of people actually harmed by a defective product is finite.

**Assessment:** The demand elasticity rating of 4 is correct. Jevons paradox is genuinely operating in mass tort — AI is expanding the addressable market by (a) identifying harms earlier, (b) lowering intake costs enough to pursue previously marginal claims, and (c) making cases more attractive to litigation funders. But "4" should not be read as "unlimited" — judicial and legislative constraints are emerging. The current description claiming firms "screen 100,000+ potential claimants annually" is plausible but unsourced. The description should emphasize the specific mechanisms through which demand expansion is occurring.

### Could AI-powered defense tools neutralize the plaintiff-side advantage?

**Not in the near term.**

The research found no defense-specific AI tools comparable to Darrow, Supio, Justpoint, or EvenUp. Defense-side mass tort firms face the same structural barriers as insurance defense: their clients (pharmaceutical companies, device manufacturers) control spending and are incentivized to minimize legal costs, not invest in defense innovation.

The real constraint on plaintiff-side growth comes not from defense AI but from:
1. **Judicial quality controls** — heightened pleading standards, sanctions, mandatory early disclosure
2. **Legislative reform** — proposals for mandatory Rule 11 sanctions and PSC quality standards
3. **Morgan & Morgan-style AI failures** — three Morgan & Morgan attorneys were sanctioned for filing AI-generated citations to nonexistent cases, illustrating the reputational and legal risk of careless AI deployment

**Assessment:** The current profile does not discuss defense-side AI threats, and it does not need to. The asymmetry is real. Self-service resistance ratings of 4 for both junior and senior are well-supported.
